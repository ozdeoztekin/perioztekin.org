NeuroSense.

A three-layer educational content pipeline that translates peer-reviewed neuroscience research into age-appropriate short-form content, without compromising mechanistic accuracy.

What this is

A three-layer pipeline for science communication that doesn’t cheat.

NeuroSense is an independent research and content design project. The aim is to support neuroscience literacy in pre-college audiences without compromising mechanistic accuracy — the kind of work that sits at the intersection of primary research and translational science communication.

The pipeline draws on peer-reviewed and primary-source neuroscience material (starting with Huberman Lab Podcast transcripts) and outputs short-form video content built for younger learners. Three architectural layers enforce scientific fidelity:

  • Atomic claim extraction with timestamps. Each scientific claim becomes a discrete unit anchored to its verbatim source quote and exact timestamp.
  • Constrained vocabulary simplification that preserves causal and temporal logic. Translation happens at the word level only. The mechanism and its causal structure pass through unchanged.
  • Annotated metaphor layers with explicit transformation notes. Every visual or narrative metaphor carries documentation explaining how it maps onto the underlying mechanism — and where it would distort if pushed too far.

The output: age-tiered short-form content where every claim traces back to a primary source, and every metaphor is accountable for what it does and doesn’t represent.

The pipeline

Master Automation Protocol · v1.0.

Phase 1 — Truth Layer (ingestion & atomic extraction)

Each transcript is parsed into Claim Cards. Every card carries: the verbatim source quote, the timestamp, the underlying mechanism in plain English, the action implication, and the citations the source itself referenced. Hard rule: if the original source did not say it, it does not get a card.

Phase 2 — Access Layer (constrained simplification)

Each Claim Card produces four scripts — one per age tier — under one rule: translate the vocabulary, never the logic. The causal and temporal structure of the source survives unchanged from preschool to high school; only word choice and the depth of unpacking changes.

Phase 3 — Relevance Layer (action mapping)

Each tier gets one concrete action grounded in the source claim. The No-Invention Rule: no fabricated gamified rewards (“Sleep gives you +10 gold”); only visualizations of the actual mechanism (“Sleep repairs the bridge so you can cross faster”).

Phase 4 — Engaging Layer (production)

Each script becomes a storyboard with scene description, action, on-screen text (3–5 words max), camera direction, and avatar gestures. Voice is consistent neural TTS — one calm, distinct voice per tier. Music is lo-fi instrumental, background only.

Phase 5 — Trust Layer (audit trail)

Every video is paired with a public Source Log: a four-column document mapping what the video said to what the source said verbatim, with timestamps and links. Verification path from any claim to primary literature is one click.

The audience tiers

Four locked archetypes, one causal structure.

Preschool · ages 4–6

Cozy Helper · Nori

Translation logic: routine and comfort. Focus on safety and feeling good.

Soft, round, slow. A helper creature named Nori. The mechanism becomes a routine the child can recognize and copy: say hello to the sun, cozy your eyes, rest your bridge.

Elementary · ages 7–10

Sandbox Builder · Theo / Alexa

Translation logic: building and energy. Focus on battery charge and speed.

Blocks, crafting, tangible cause-and-effect. The mechanism becomes a system you can construct: charge your solar battery, repair the path, top off the engine.

Middle school · ages 11–13

Quest / Obby · Kai

Translation logic: skill and grind. Focus on leveling up and reducing lag.

Levels, obstacles, stylized challenge. The mechanism becomes a skill tree: unlock the focus buff, cool down the lag, level up the lookout.

High school · ages 14–18

Sim Dashboard · first-person “You”

Translation logic: systems and metrics. Focus on optimization and stats.

Sliders, UI, instrumentation. The mechanism becomes a dashboard the viewer is operating: calibrate the circadian timer, tune the alpha/theta ratio, watch the recovery curve.

Why this matters

Most science content for kids dilutes the science. Most content for adults skips the audience.

Edutainment for young children turns mechanism into spectacle and loses the actual claim. Long-form science podcasts assume an adult vocabulary, an adult attention budget, and adult prior knowledge. Younger audiences who could understand the science — if it were translated honestly — get either watered-down clichés or nothing.

NeuroSense is the project I built to close that gap with a workflow rather than a guess. The protocol is the deliverable. If it works, it scales.
