Coming soon · founder project

NeuroSense.

Real neuroscience research, translated into four age tiers without diluting the science. Every claim verifiable. Every metaphor checked.

Why this exists

Most science content for kids dilutes the science. Most content for adults skips the audience.

Edutainment for young children turns mechanism into spectacle and loses the actual claim. Long-form science podcasts assume an adult vocabulary, an adult’s attention budget, and adult prior knowledge. Younger audiences who could understand the science — if it were translated honestly — get either watered-down clichés or nothing.

NeuroSense fills that gap with a locked protocol: every video starts from a primary source, extracts the actual mechanism, and translates it — vocabulary only, never logic — into four age tiers. Visuals illustrate mechanism. Every metaphor is checked against the science it represents. Every episode comes with a public source log so the audit trail is part of the product.

The protocol

NeuroSense Master Automation Protocol · v1.0.

Phase 1 — Truth Layer (ingestion & extraction)

Every advice or mechanism claim becomes a structured Claim Card with the verbatim source quote, timestamp, mechanism in plain English, the action implication, and the citations the source itself referenced. Hard rule: if the original source did not say it, it does not get a card. No external Googling. No filling gaps with plausible-sounding speculation.

Phase 2 — Access Layer (translation & tiering)

Each Claim Card produces four scripts — one per age tier — under one rule: translate the vocabulary, never the logic. The causal structure of the source survives unchanged from preschool to high school; only word choice and the depth of unpacking changes.

Phase 3 — Relevance Layer (action mapping)

Each tier gets one concrete action grounded in the source claim. The No-Invention Rule: no invented gamified rewards (“Sleep gives you +10 gold”); only visualizations of the actual mechanism (“Sleep repairs the bridge so you can cross faster”).

Phase 4 — Engaging Layer (production)

Each script becomes a storyboard with scene description, action, on-screen text (3–5 words max), camera direction, and avatar gestures. Voice is consistent neural TTS — one calm, distinct voice per tier. No “kiddy” voices. Music is lo-fi instrumental, background only.

Phase 5 — Trust Layer (audit trail)

Every video comes with a public “NeuroSense Source Log” that maps what the video said to what the source said verbatim, with timestamps and links. If a viewer, parent, teacher, or scientist wants to verify any claim, the path from claim to primary literature is one click long.

The audience tiers

Four locked archetypes, one causal structure.

Preschool · ages 4–6

Cozy Helper · Nori

Translation logic: routine and comfort. Focus on safety and feeling good.

Soft, round, slow. A helper creature named Nori. The mechanism becomes a routine the child can recognize and copy: say hello to the sun, cozy your eyes, rest your bridge.

Elementary · ages 7–10

Sandbox Builder · Theo / Alexa

Translation logic: building and energy. Focus on battery charge and speed.

Blocks, crafting, tangible cause-and-effect. The mechanism becomes a system you can construct: charge your solar battery, repair the path, top off the engine.

Middle school · ages 11–13

Quest / Obby · Kai

Translation logic: skill and grind. Focus on leveling up and reducing lag.

Levels, obstacles, stylized challenge. The mechanism becomes a skill tree: unlock the focus buff, cool down the lag, level up the lookout.

High school · ages 14–18

Sim Dashboard · first-person “You”

Translation logic: systems and metrics. Focus on optimization and stats.

Sliders, UI, instrumentation. The mechanism becomes a dashboard the viewer is operating: calibrate the circadian timer, tune the alpha/theta ratio, watch the recovery curve.

Audit philosophy

Source logs as a feature, not an afterthought.

Most science communication asks for trust. NeuroSense earns it on the line. Every published video is paired with a public Source Log document containing four columns:

  • Column A. What the video said, sentence by sentence.
  • Column B. What the original source said, verbatim.
  • Column C. Timestamp and link to the source.
  • Column D. Scientific references the source itself cited.
