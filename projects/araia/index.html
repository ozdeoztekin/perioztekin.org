A.R.A.I.A.

An AI-powered aphasia rehabilitation app for stroke survivors. Personalized, evidence-based speech therapy delivered through clinically-supported techniques and accessibility-first design. Tagline: Empowering Every Word.

The problem

Aphasia: when the words go but the mind stays.

Aphasia is a language disorder, most often caused by stroke, that damages the left-hemisphere networks responsible for producing or comprehending speech. The condition does not touch intelligence. People with aphasia know exactly what they want to say. They simply cannot reach for it.

Conventional speech therapy is effective but resource-bound: weekly clinic appointments, a therapist’s schedule, transportation, costs that scale with severity. Most patients receive only a fraction of the practice their recovering circuitry actually needs. A.R.A.I.A. addresses that gap with a phone in the pocket and a therapy protocol grounded in primary research.

The science

Melodic Intonation Therapy.

The mechanism A.R.A.I.A. operationalizes is Melodic Intonation Therapy (MIT): a clinically validated method that uses melody, rhythm, and tapping to recruit intact right-hemisphere language analogues to compensate for damaged left-hemisphere areas. Patients who can no longer speak words can often sing them, because the neural substrate for melodic prosody runs through pathways the stroke did not destroy.

A.R.A.I.A. is built around five evidence-based modules: paced repetition, picture-naming with melodic prompting, conversational practice with AI partners, daily structured practice routines, and progress tracking calibrated to MIT outcome measures from the published literature. Every clinical claim in the app traces back to a peer-reviewed source verified during development.

The design

Built for users whose attention and frustration tolerance are under strain.

Big targets. Calm color. One thing per screen. Voice input as a first-class peer to text. Progress visible without being demanding.

The team

A six-person capstone.

A.R.A.I.A. was built collaboratively by a team of six CNI-X students with equal contributions across research, clinical-claim verification, design, and presentation. As the youngest student admitted to the cohort, contributing equally to a senior-led team meant carrying my own weight on the science: every health claim I wrote traced back to a primary source I had read and verified myself.

Where this could go

From concept to clinic.

A.R.A.I.A. is currently a high-fidelity concept and prototype. The next steps that would push it toward real-world use:

  • Pilot with a small cohort of post-stroke aphasia patients in partnership with a speech-language pathology clinic.
  • IRB review and outcome-measure validation against established MIT trial benchmarks.
  • Voice-fluency assessment using on-device ML (privacy-preserving) instead of cloud round-trips.
  • Family / caregiver companion view — aphasia recovery is a household project, not a solo one.
