About

The throughline

I have always been drawn to the question underneath every science class I have taken: not just what happens, but why the body and mind bother. Why does a neuron fire at all? Why does a brain, damaged and disoriented, sometimes find its way back?

Honors Chemistry gave me molecular language. Honors Biology showed me that living systems are built from restlessness — membrane potentials flickering, enzymes reading signals, cells responding to a world they can’t see. Honors Physics gave me the forces underneath both. Each course pointed me toward the same place.

What draws me to neuroscience is that it sits at the intersection I have been trying to reach: the place where cellular mechanisms become human experience — where a misfiring synapse becomes a lost memory or a changed personality, and where neuroplasticity becomes clinical recovery.

I don’t treat my courses as separate tracks. I look for the connections: the electrochemical gradients in chemistry that map onto the ion channels in biology, the signal propagation in neurons that follows the wave mechanics of physics. Each course gives me discipline; together they give me direction. I am the kind of student who builds the curriculum when one does not exist.

Stanford CNI-X 2025 Capstone

Voicify

AI-driven rehabilitation app for aphasia · Co-led capstone team

An AI-powered speech-recovery app for stroke survivors with aphasia — people whose minds remain intact but whose words have been taken away. Voicify operationalizes Melodic Intonation Therapy, recruiting intact right-hemisphere networks to compensate for damaged language areas. Neuroplasticity made clinical.

  • Designed therapy modules grounded in published MIT protocols
  • Built interactive demo (React/TSX prototype + Canva slide deck)
  • Verified each clinical claim against primary aphasia literature
  • Presented to Stanford CNI-X cohort & faculty in final demo
Independent Research Concept

NeuroCalm

Brainwave-responsive headband for sensory regulation · Sole researcher & designer

A wearable that listens to alpha (8–12 Hz) and theta (4–7 Hz) rhythms and gives the brain a signal — a calming light, a gentle vibration — before sensory overload becomes crisis. Designed with autistic individuals in mind, useful to anyone who can’t always speak overload in real time.

  • Reviewed the neurofeedback literature (Coben 2010, Kouijzer 2009, Thompson 2010 et al.)
  • Specified architecture: NeuroSky MindWave Mobile 2 + Arduino + LED/haptic feedback
  • Wrote prototype Arduino C++ firmware with simulated EEG fallback
  • Framed transparently as experimental: the evidence is promising, not settled

NeuroSense — neuroscience that scales by age, not by hand-waving

Most science communication for kids dilutes the science. Most communication for adults skips the audience. NeuroSense will do neither. The protocol is locked: every video starts from a primary source (transcripts of working scientists, beginning with the Huberman Lab), extracts the actual mechanism, then translates it — only the vocabulary, never the logic — into four age-tiered scripts. Every metaphor is checked against the science it represents, and every episode ships with a public source log so the audit trail is part of the product.

Step 1 · Truth Layer

Claim Cards

Every advice claim gets a card with timestamp, verbatim text, mechanism, and Huberman’s cited papers. If it isn’t in the source, it doesn’t make the video.

Step 2 · Access Layer

Four Age Tiers

Preschool (Nori), elementary (Theo), middle (Kai), high (first-person sim). Same causal logic; vocabulary and visuals scale.

Step 3 · Trust Layer

Public Source Log

Every video paired with a published audit trail: what we said, what the source said verbatim, where to verify. Trust is engineered, not asked for.
