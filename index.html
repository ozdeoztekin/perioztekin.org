About me

Why neuroscience.

“I have been fascinated by every science class I have taken and couldn’t help but wonder not just what happens, but why the body and mind bother. Why does a neuron fire at all? Why does a brain, damaged and disoriented, sometimes find its way back? — from my Stanford application essay

Honors Chemistry gave me a molecular language: electrons shifting, bonds breaking, energy moving through a system looking for equilibrium. Honors Biology showed me that living systems are built from that same restlessness — membrane potentials flickering, enzymes reading signals, cells responding to a world they can’t see. Honors Physics gave me the forces that underlie both. Each class kept pointing me toward the same place. The brain.

What draws me most is the place where cellular mechanisms become human experience: where a misfiring synapse turns into a lost memory or a changed personality, and where neuroplasticity becomes clinical recovery. I want to study the conditions under which the brain fails and the conditions under which it comes back, not as separate phenomena but as two sides of the same plasticity.

“I do not stop at the edge of one subject. I follow the question wherever it goes. — from the same essay

Featured project

Voicify — AI rehab for aphasia

Voicify is a speech-recovery app for stroke survivors with aphasia, the condition where the words are gone but the mind remains. We grounded it in Melodic Intonation Therapy: melody and rhythm recruit intact right-hemisphere networks to compensate for damaged left-hemisphere ones. Neuroplasticity, made clinical. Built in two weeks at Stanford CNI-X 2025.
