About me

Why neuroscience.

“There is a significant gap between studying the brain and engaging with real-time clinical questions. At Stanford’s Clinical Neuroscience Immersion Experience (CNI-X), I saw that gap close. — from my Summer of Neurosciences statement

Honors Chemistry gave me a molecular language: electrons shifting, bonds breaking, energy moving through a system looking for equilibrium. Honors Biology showed me that living systems are built from that same restlessness. Honors Physics gave me the forces that underlie both. Each class kept pointing me toward the same place. The brain.

Co-developing A.R.A.I.A., the AI-powered aphasia rehabilitation tool that became our Stanford CNI-X capstone, required diving into the literature on Melodic Intonation Therapy and post-stroke neuroplasticity. It is one thing to interpret a study’s empirical validity; it is another to hold that data against the patient in the next room. I have trained myself to read with that focus ever since.

What draws me most to neuroscience is that the brain’s failure and recovery express the same underlying plasticity. I want to study the conditions under which it fails and the conditions under which it comes back, not as separate phenomena but as two sides of the same mechanism.

“My HarvardX coursework built a strong foundation in neurobiology, but it has been a solitary pursuit. I have taught myself to read primary literature carefully; now I want to read it in conversation. — from the same statement

What I’ve been working on.

Featured project

A.R.A.I.A. — AI rehab for aphasia

A.R.A.I.A. is a speech-recovery app for stroke survivors with aphasia, the condition where the words are gone but the mind remains. We grounded it in Melodic Intonation Therapy: melody and rhythm recruit intact right-hemisphere networks to compensate for damaged left-hemisphere ones. Built in two weeks at Stanford CNI-X 2025 with a six-person team.
